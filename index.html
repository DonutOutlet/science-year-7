Understanding how scientific ideas change over time

🔭 Pluto and Changing Scientific Ideas

Pluto used to be known as the ninth planet. However, new telescope technology in the 1990s and early 2000s helped scientists discover the Kuiper Belt, a large region filled with icy objects beyond Neptune.

Scientists realised Pluto was not unique and was actually one of many similar objects. Another object called Eris was even larger than Pluto. Because Pluto had not cleared its orbit of other objects, scientists reclassified it as a dwarf planet in 2006.

☀️ Heliocentric Model

The heliocentric model was proposed by Nicolaus Copernicus in 1543. He published his ideas in a book called De revolutionibus orbium coelestium.

Instead of Earth being at the centre, Copernicus suggested that the Sun was at the centre of the solar system.

His evidence was based on mathematical calculations and observations using the naked eye. His model also better explained why planets sometimes appear to move backwards in the sky.

🌏 Indigenous Knowledge of Astronomy

Mallee Fowl

The eggs of the Mallee Fowl provided food for generations, while the bird itself held cultural importance and helped improve quality of life.

Dingoes

Dingoes were used as companions and helped with hunting and protection. They also have strong cultural significance.

Baidam (Shark Constellation)

This constellation helped mark important seasonal changes, such as when to fish, hunt, or plant. It also helped with navigation.

Harvesting Ants

Ant activity was used as a natural indicator for food collection and environmental changes.

✨ Observing the Sky

Stellar Scintillation

Stars appear to twinkle due to Earth's atmosphere. This effect helped people identify constellations.

Emu in the Sky

A dark constellation used by Aboriginal Australians to determine the right time to collect emu eggs.

Rakai Harvest

A sedge tuber collected by Arnhem Land Aboriginal people during the wet season.
